CHAPTER ONE: INTRODUCTION
1.0 BACKGROUND TO THE STUDY
Urban air pollution poses a severe public health crisis in Nigerian cities, particularly Lagos, where fine particulate matter (PM2.5, PM10, PM1) concentrations frequently exceed WHO guidelines. Rapid urbanization, heavy traffic, industrial activities, generator emissions, and seasonal Harmattan dust create complex spatial-temporal pollution patterns that traditional monitoring struggles to characterize effectively.
Recent community sensor networks like AirQo have generated rich datasets containing hourly PM1, PM2.5, PM10 measurements alongside temperature and humidity from multiple Lagos locations. However, these valuable datasets remain underutilized, typically summarized through basic statistics that obscure underlying pollution regimes, hotspots, and anomalous events.
Unsupervised machine learning offers powerful tools to discover latent patterns in such unlabeled environmental data. Clustering algorithms—K-Means for compact groups, DBSCAN for density-based patterns and noise handling, and hierarchical clustering for structural insights—can systematically identify "clean air days," "traffic-dominated periods," "Harmattan dust episodes," and sensor anomalies without requiring labeled training data.
This study applies these techniques to Lagos hourly sensor data (2024) to reveal actionable pollution patterns supporting targeted urban air quality interventions.
1.1 PROBLEM STATEMENT
Current air quality monitoring in Lagos produces fragmented datasets that fail to reveal underlying spatial-temporal pollution patterns essential for effective intervention. Traditional approaches rely on summary statistics and threshold exceedance reports, missing complex regimes such as:
Diurnal traffic peaks vs. baseline pollution
Harmattan dust episodes vs. local combustion sources
Station-specific hotspots vs. city-wide trends
Sensor noise vs. genuine pollution spikes
The newly available hourly sensor data (PM1, PM2.5, PM10, meteorological variables from multiple locations) represents a step forward, but lacks systematic analysis to extract meaningful patterns. No prior Nigerian research has applied comparative unsupervised clustering (K-Means, DBSCAN, hierarchical) to such rich, multi-sensor datasets to characterize urban pollution regimes.
This creates a critical gap between raw sensor data collection and actionable insights for policymakers, health authorities, and urban planners who need spatially and temporally resolved pollution patterns to prioritize interventions effectively.
1.2 AIM AND OBJECTIVES
Aim: To apply unsupervised machine learning clustering to Lagos multi-sensor air quality data to discover and characterize spatial-temporal pollution patterns.
Specific Objectives:
To preprocess and integrate hourly PM1, PM2.5, PM10 data from multiple Lagos sensors into analysis-ready daily and hourly feature sets.
To perform exploratory analysis and dimensionality reduction (PCA) to understand data structure.
To apply and compare K-Means, DBSCAN, and hierarchical clustering algorithms using internal validation metrics.
To visualize discovered pollution regimes through time series, PCA scatter plots, cluster profile heatmaps, and spatial sensor maps.
To interpret clusters in context of known Lagos emission sources, meteorology, and seasonal patterns.

CHAPTER TWO: LITERATURE REVIEW
2.0 INTRODUCTION
This chapter reviews literature essential to the study, focusing on air quality challenges in developing urban contexts, limitations of traditional analysis methods, and the application of unsupervised machine learning clustering to environmental sensor data. The review establishes the theoretical foundation for applying K-Means, DBSCAN, and hierarchical clustering to Lagos hourly PM1, PM2.5, and PM10 datasets, identifies research gaps in Nigerian contexts, and presents a conceptual framework guiding the methodology. By synthesizing global applications with local data realities, this chapter justifies the comparative clustering approach central to the research.
2.1 AIR QUALITY MONITORING CHALLENGES IN DEVELOPING COUNTRIES
2.1.1 The Global Burden and Local Manifestations
Urban air pollution has emerged as one of the most pressing public health crises of the 21st century, with the burden disproportionately borne by populations in low- and middle-income countries. The World Health Organization estimates that approximately 80% of pollution-related disease burden affects these regions, where rapid urbanization outpaces environmental infrastructure development (WHO, 2019).
Nigeria, and particularly its economic capital Lagos, exemplifies these challenges in acute form. As one of Africa's fastest-growing megacities, Lagos faces a confluence of pollution sources that create a complex and dynamic air quality landscape. Rapid and often unplanned urbanization has concentrated populations and activities in ways that maximize pollutant exposure. The city's infamous traffic congestion—with vehicles spending an average of 30 hours per week in gridlock—generates massive quantities of vehicular emissions in poorly ventilated street canyons (Paterne et al., 2022).
Beyond traffic, Lagos's air quality is shaped by multiple contributing factors. Industrial emissions and informal manufacturing clusters release pollutants with minimal filtration or control. The near-ubiquitous use of diesel and petrol generators for backup power, necessitated by unreliable electricity supply, creates distributed emission sources that pervade residential and commercial areas alike. Seasonal phenomena add further complexity: the Harmattan, a dry and dusty northeasterly trade wind occurring from November to March, transports fine desert particles from the Sahara, dramatically elevating particulate matter concentrations (Paterne et al., 2022).
2.1.2 The Monitoring Infrastructure Paradox
Traditional regulatory air quality monitoring in Nigeria relies on a sparse network of reference-grade monitoring stations operated by the National Environmental Standards and Regulations Enforcement Agency (NESREA). Infrastructure deficit (approximately 0.4 stations per million people compared to 10 or more in North American cities) creates vast spatial blind spots where pollution levels remain unknown (NASA, 2014).s
This scarcity has profound implications. Regulatory decisions based on data from one or two centrally-located monitors may fail to capture the significant spatial heterogeneity characteristic of urban air quality. Industrial areas, traffic corridors, residential neighborhoods, and coastal zones can exhibit dramatically different pollution profiles, yet remain indistinguishable in aggregated city-level statistics. 
The emergence of low-cost Internet-of-Things (IoT) sensor networks represents both opportunity and challenge. Initiatives like AirQo, developed by Makerere University and deployed across African cities including Lagos, have introduced networks of low-cost sensors capable of generating dense, high-frequency measurements at a fraction of traditional costs. These sensors can be deployed at spatial densities impossible with reference monitors, potentially providing unprecedented insights into hyperlocal pollution patterns (Buelvas et al., 2023; Stratoulias et al., 2024).
However, the cost savings come with methodological trade-offs. Low-cost sensors exhibit greater susceptibility to calibration drift, environmental interference, and measurement noise compared to reference instruments. Humidity, temperature extremes, and cross-sensitivities to non-target gases can introduce systematic and random errors. Data quality challenges—including missing values from power outages, communication failures, and sensor malfunctions—are more prevalent than in regulatory networks (Buelvas et al., 2023). These characteristics necessitate sophisticated analytical approaches capable of extracting meaningful patterns from imperfect data.

2.1.3 Pollutants and Variables of Interest
This study focuses on three particulate matter size fractions that provide complementary information about pollution sources and health risks. The dataset employs abbreviated nomenclature: P0 represents PM1 (particles ≤1 μm in aerodynamic diameter), P1 represents PM2.5 (particles ≤2.5 μm), and P2 represents PM10 (particles ≤10 μm).
PM2.5 (P1) constitutes the fraction of greatest health concern. These fine particles, less than 2.5 micrometers in diameter—approximately 30 times smaller than a human hair—can penetrate deep into the alveolar regions of the lungs and even enter the bloodstream. Epidemiological studies have linked PM2.5 exposure to cardiovascular disease, respiratory illness, cognitive impairment, and premature mortality (Jonathan et al., 2011). In urban environments, PM2.5 originates primarily from combustion processes: vehicle exhaust, generator emissions, biomass burning, and industrial operations.
PM10 (P2) includes PM2.5 plus coarser particles up to 10 micrometers in diameter. While these larger particles typically deposit in the upper respiratory tract rather than reaching deep lung tissue, they remain health-relevant and provide important source information. The PM10 fraction captures road dust resuspended by traffic, construction activities, and in Lagos's context, Harmattan-transported desert dust. The ratio of PM2.5 to PM10 can help distinguish between combustion-dominated and dust-dominated pollution episodes.
PM1 (P0), the finest fraction, represents ultrafine and accumulation-mode particles predominantly from combustion. While less commonly monitored than PM2.5 and PM10, PM1 provides additional source discrimination capability and may be particularly relevant for traffic-related exposures.
Beyond particulate matter, the dataset includes meteorological co-variables—particularly temperature and relative humidity—that profoundly influence pollutant concentrations and behavior. Temperature affects pollutant dispersion through its role in atmospheric stability and mixing height. Humidity influences particle hygroscopic growth, affecting both mass concentrations and sensor response. Temporal features, including hour of day and season, enable identification of pollution regimes linked to traffic patterns and meteorological cycles.
2.1.4 Data Complexity and Volume
The hourly, multi-sensor nature of IoT air quality networks generates datasets of considerable complexity. Across multiple sensor locations in Lagos, continuous hourly measurements of three pollutant fractions plus meteorological variables create spatiotemporal datasets comprising tens of thousands of observations. Each observation represents a point in multi-dimensional feature space defined by pollution concentrations, meteorological conditions, spatial coordinates, and temporal attributes.
This data richness enables sophisticated pattern discovery but also demands robust analytical frameworks. Simple aggregation to daily or monthly averages discards the temporal resolution necessary to identify diurnal cycles or episodic events. Spatial averaging obscures neighborhood-scale heterogeneity. Missing data from individual sensors requires thoughtful imputation strategies that preserve underlying patterns without introducing artifacts.
These characteristics position low-cost sensor networks as "big data" sources requiring methods fundamentally different from traditional monitoring approaches designed around sparse, high-quality measurements. The following sections explore why conventional analytical frameworks prove inadequate and how unsupervised machine learning addresses these limitations.
2.2 LIMITATIONS OF TRADITIONAL AIR QUALITY ANALYSIS METHODS
The analytical approaches conventionally applied to air quality data emerged in an era of sparse regulatory monitoring and were optimized for different data characteristics than those of modern sensor networks. This section critically examines three traditional approaches and identifies their fundamental limitations when applied to rich IoT datasets.
2.2.1 Statistical Time Series Methods
Classical statistical approaches, including linear regression, ARIMA models, and generalized additive models, have formed the backbone of air quality analysis for decades. These methods assume functional relationships between pollutants and predictors that can be characterized through parametric models. However, these assumptions prove problematic for urban air quality systems where pollutant-meteorology relationships are profoundly nonlinear and emissions exhibit non-stationary behavior across hourly, daily, and seasonal cycles (Qiu et al., 2022).
Furthermore, threshold-based approaches impose artificial categories on continuous phenomena, missing latent structure in the data. Pollution episodes may cluster into distinct regimes—Harmattan dust events, morning traffic peaks, evening generator use—with different source profiles and spatial patterns, but these regimes lack predefined threshold boundaries (Karatzas et al., 2018). Statistical methods require the analyst to specify categories a priori, precluding discovery of unexpected patterns.
2.2.2 Chemical Transport Models
Chemical transport models (CTMs) such as CMAQ and WRF-Chem represent the mechanistic end of the analytical spectrum, solving coupled differential equations describing pollutant emissions, atmospheric transport, chemical transformation, and deposition (Zhaoqi et al., 2023). While providing process-level insights, CTMs require detailed inputs unavailable for cities like Lagos: gridded emissions inventories, three-dimensional meteorological fields, and extensive chemical mechanism parameterizations that demand months of effort and significant resources to develop.
Even when inputs can be assembled, CTMs demand substantial computational resources and are designed as forward models rather than inverse tools for interpreting observed sensor data. While data assimilation techniques exist to incorporate observations, they add further complexity and computational burden, making CTMs unwieldy for real-time sensor data analysis or exploratory pattern discovery (Nault et al., 2021).
2.2.3 Descriptive Statistical Reporting
The most common approach to air quality data analysis involves descriptive statistics: calculating daily or monthly averages, counting exceedances of air quality standards, and comparing values across time periods or locations. While accessible and easily communicated, purely descriptive approaches fail to exploit the information content of rich sensor datasets, with spatial averaging obscuring neighborhood-scale heterogeneity and temporal averaging eliminating diurnal structure that could identify dominant source contributions (Hoffman et al., 2022).
Critically, descriptive statistics provide no framework for discovering latent structure. If pollution episodes cluster into distinct regimes with different meteorological signatures, spatial patterns, and health implications, descriptive methods offer no pathway to identify them, requiring the analyst to specify all comparisons and groupings in advance and precluding data-driven discovery of unexpected patterns.
2.3 UNSUPERVISED LEARNING: THEORETICAL FOUNDATIONS
2.3.1 The Unsupervised Learning Paradigm
Machine learning tasks broadly divide into supervised and unsupervised categories based on the availability of labeled training data. Supervised learning requires a dataset where the outcome of interest is known for each observation, enabling the algorithm to learn predictive relationships. Unsupervised learning, in contrast, operates on unlabeled data, seeking to discover inherent structure without predefined categories or target variables (GeeksforGeeks, 2025a).
For air quality analysis, this distinction proves crucial. While one might possess thousands of hourly pollution measurements, each accompanied by meteorological conditions and temporal markers, these observations typically lack authoritative labels identifying their "type." Unsupervised learning addresses precisely this scenario: discovering natural groupings, patterns, or structures that exist in the data but have not been explicitly labeled by human annotators.
2.3.2 Core Unsupervised Learning Techniques
The unsupervised learning toolkit encompasses several complementary approaches, each addressing different analytical needs:
Clustering algorithms group similar observations based on feature similarity. In the air quality context, clustering might group hours with similar pollution profiles, sensors with similar temporal patterns, or days with similar meteorological-pollution regimes. Clustering forms the primary focus of this study and receives detailed treatment in Section 2.4.
Dimensionality reduction techniques project high-dimensional data into lower-dimensional spaces while preserving essential structure. Principal Component Analysis (PCA), the most widely used approach, identifies orthogonal linear combinations of original variables (principal components) that capture maximum variance. For air quality datasets with 10-15 variables (multiple pollutant fractions, meteorological parameters, temporal features), PCA can project observations into two or three dimensions for visualization while retaining most information (GeeksforGeeks, 2025f). This proves invaluable for visual validation of clustering results and identification of dominant patterns.
Anomaly detection methods identify observations that deviate significantly from typical patterns. In air quality applications, anomalies might represent extreme pollution episodes, sensor malfunctions, or novel pollution regimes not previously observed. While not central to this study, anomaly detection complements clustering by highlighting observations that resist categorization.

2.3.3 Cluster Validation Without Ground Truth
A fundamental challenge in unsupervised learning involves evaluating algorithm performance without ground truth labels. If the true cluster assignments are unknown—the scenario motivating unsupervised approaches—how can one assess whether discovered clusters are "good"?
Cluster validation metrics address this question through internal evaluation: quantifying cluster quality using only the data and cluster assignments themselves, without reference to external labels. Several complementary metrics have been developed, each capturing different aspects of cluster quality:
Silhouette Score measures how similar each point is to its own cluster compared to other clusters (Rousseeuw, 1987). For each observation, the silhouette value is calculated as:
s = (b - a) / max(a, b)
where a is the mean distance to other points in the same cluster (cohesion), and b is the mean distance to points in the nearest neighboring cluster (separation). The silhouette coefficient ranges from -1 to +1, with values near +1 indicating the point is well-matched to its cluster and poorly-matched to neighbors, values near 0 indicating the point lies near cluster boundaries, and negative values suggesting potential misclassification. The overall silhouette score averages across all points, providing a single metric of clustering quality. Higher values indicate better-defined, more separated clusters.
Davies-Bouldin Index evaluates clustering through the ratio of within-cluster scatter to between-cluster separation (Davies & Bouldin, 1979). For each cluster, the index identifies the most similar other cluster and calculates the ratio of their combined within-cluster distances to their between-center distance. The Davies-Bouldin Index averages these worst-case ratios across clusters. Lower values indicate better clustering, with well-separated, compact clusters yielding values approaching zero. Unlike silhouette score, the Davies-Bouldin Index emphasizes cluster compactness and does not penalize overlapping clusters as severely.
Calinski-Harabasz Index, also known as the Variance Ratio Criterion, quantifies the ratio of between-cluster variance to within-cluster variance (Caliński & Harabasz, 1974). Higher values indicate better-defined clusters, as they suggest greater separation between cluster centers relative to dispersion within clusters. This metric implicitly favors dense, well-separated clusters and can be computed efficiently even for large datasets.
Importantly, these metrics can disagree: an algorithm might achieve high silhouette scores but modest Calinski-Harabasz values, or vice versa. This disagreement reflects different implicit assumptions about cluster quality. Best practice involves examining multiple metrics alongside domain knowledge: do the discovered clusters align with known pollution regimes? Do they exhibit interpretable meteorological or temporal signatures? Quantitative validation metrics guide algorithm selection, but domain interpretability ultimately determines utility (GeeksforGeeks, 2025i).
2.3.4 The Role of Feature Engineering and Preprocessing
Unsupervised learning algorithms operate on features—the variables describing each observation. For air quality data, feature selection and engineering profoundly influence results. Should temperature be included as a clustering variable, or only pollutant concentrations? Should hours of the day be encoded as continuous variables (0-23), or as sine and cosine transformations to preserve circular continuity (hour 23 is adjacent to hour 0)? Should spatial coordinates be included, or analyzed separately?
Feature scaling also proves critical. Most clustering algorithms use distance metrics to quantify similarity, and unscaled features with large numerical ranges (PM10 concentrations might range 0-500 μg/m³) will dominate distance calculations over small-range features (relative humidity 0-100%). Standardization—transforming each feature to zero mean and unit variance—ensures all variables contribute proportionally to similarity calculations, though at the cost of obscuring original units.
These preprocessing decisions, while technical, embody analytical choices about what constitutes "similarity" in the application context. The methodology chapter details specific choices for this study, but their importance merits emphasis in this theoretical foundation.
2.4 CLUSTERING ALGORITHMS: PRINCIPLES, APPLICATIONS, AND COMPARATIVE PERFORMANCE
This section examines three clustering algorithms—K-Means, DBSCAN, and hierarchical clustering—that represent complementary approaches to pattern discovery. Each embodies different assumptions about cluster shape, density, and structure, leading to distinct strengths and limitations.
2.4.1 K-Means Clustering: Partitional Simplicity
K-Means, developed by Stuart Lloyd in 1957 and refined by MacQueen in 1967, remains perhaps the most widely used clustering algorithm due to its computational efficiency and interpretable results. The algorithm seeks to partition n observations into K pre-specified clusters by minimizing within-cluster sum of squared distances to cluster centers.
Algorithmic Procedure:
 The K-Means algorithm follows an iterative refinement process (GeeksforGeeks, 2025b):
Initialization: Select K initial cluster centers (centroids), either randomly from the data points or through more sophisticated initialization like K-Means++
Assignment Step: Assign each observation to the nearest centroid based on Euclidean distance
Update Step: Recalculate each centroid as the mean of all points assigned to that cluster
Iteration: Repeat assignment and update steps until convergence (centroids stop moving) or a maximum iteration limit is reached
Strengths in Air Quality Applications:
 K-Means offers several advantages for pollution data analysis. Its computational efficiency—O(nKt) where t is iterations—enables application to large sensor datasets with tens of thousands of hourly observations. The algorithm produces highly interpretable results: each cluster centroid represents a "typical" pollution profile for that regime, with centroid values directly interpretable in original units (e.g., PM2.5 = 45 μg/m³, temperature = 28°C, hour = 18). This interpretability facilitates communication with policymakers and domain experts.
The algorithm's simplicity also constitutes an advantage: few hyperparameters require tuning (essentially only K), reducing the risk of overfitting or arbitrary parameter choices. Numerous implementations exist across programming languages and platforms, with mature, well-tested code readily available.
Limitations and Vulnerabilities:
 K-Means' elegant simplicity comes with restrictive assumptions. The algorithm assumes clusters are spherical and of roughly equal size—isotropic Gaussian distributions in feature space. Real pollution regimes often violate these assumptions: Harmattan dust events might constitute a small, dense cluster while normal traffic days form a large, diffuse cluster. K-Means will force equal partitions, potentially fragmenting the large cluster or assigning outliers to the small cluster inappropriately.
Sensitivity to outliers represents another critical limitation. Because centroids are calculated as means, a few extreme observations (sensor malfunctions reading 9999 μg/m³, for instance) can drastically shift centroids, distorting all cluster boundaries. In noisy sensor data, this proves particularly problematic.
The requirement to pre-specify K introduces a chicken-and-egg challenge: one must know the number of pollution regimes before discovering them. While heuristics like the elbow method (plotting within-cluster variance versus K and seeking an "elbow" in the curve) and silhouette analysis provide guidance, they do not eliminate the fundamental arbitrariness. Different K values can yield defensible but distinct results.
Finally, K-Means' initialization sensitivity means different random starting points can yield different final clusterings. Best practice requires multiple runs with different initializations, adding computational overhead.
2.4.2 DBSCAN: Density-Based Discovery of Arbitrary Shapes
DBSCAN (Density-Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with Noise), introduced by Ester et al. in 1996, represents a fundamentally different clustering philosophy. Rather than partitioning data into a predetermined number of spherical clusters, DBSCAN identifies regions of high density separated by regions of low density, naturally accommodating clusters of arbitrary shape and size while explicitly identifying outliers as noise.
Algorithmic Procedure:
 DBSCAN operates through a connectivity-based approach defined by two parameters (GeeksforGeeks, 2025d):
eps (ε): The radius defining a point's neighborhood
min_samples: The minimum number of points required to form a dense region
The algorithm classifies points into three categories:
Core points: Points with at least min_samples points (including themselves) within their eps-radius neighborhood
Border points: Points within the eps-neighborhood of a core point but having fewer than min_samples neighbors themselves
Noise points: Points that are neither core nor border points
Clustering proceeds by connecting core points whose neighborhoods intersect, forming density-connected regions. Border points join adjacent core points' clusters, while noise points remain unassigned.
Theoretical Advantages for Environmental Data:
 DBSCAN's density-based approach offers several critical advantages for air quality sensor data. The algorithm requires no pre-specification of cluster number—it discovers the number of regimes naturally from the data's density structure. This proves invaluable when the number of pollution regimes is genuinely unknown.
Explicit noise handling distinguishes DBSCAN from partition-based methods. In sensor data plagued by malfunctions, calibration errors, and transient anomalies, the ability to identify and exclude noise points as a distinct category rather than forcing them into clusters prevents outliers from distorting regime definitions. This robustness to noise has been repeatedly demonstrated in environmental applications (Bárbara et al., 2025).
The algorithm's ability to identify clusters of arbitrary shape proves particularly relevant for pollution regimes that may not conform to spherical assumptions. Traffic-dominated episodes might form elongated clusters in feature space (high PM2.5, moderate PM10, specific hours), while dust events form distinct compact clusters (high PM10, lower PM2.5, specific seasons). DBSCAN can identify both simultaneously, whereas K-Means might fragment the elongated cluster or merge the compact cluster with adjacent regions.
Empirical Performance Evidence:
 Recent comparative studies strongly support DBSCAN's advantages for noisy geospatial and environmental data. Bárbara et al. (2025) applied multiple clustering algorithms to geospatial datasets with measurement noise and found DBSCAN achieved a mean silhouette score of 0.8646 compared to 0.8160 for both K-Means and hierarchical clustering—a statistically significant improvement. The authors attributed this performance to DBSCAN's explicit noise handling and shape flexibility.
Indoor air quality studies have similarly demonstrated DBSCAN's superiority for identifying pollution regimes in sensor data characterized by irregular temporal patterns and measurement artifacts. The algorithm successfully distinguished between cooking events, cleaning activities, and outdoor infiltration based on multi-pollutant signatures, whereas K-Means conflated regimes with similar average concentrations but different temporal dynamics.
Parameter Sensitivity and Selection Challenges:
 DBSCAN's advantages come at the cost of parameter sensitivity. The eps and min_samples parameters profoundly influence results, yet lack universal defaults. Setting eps too small fragments natural clusters; too large, and distinct regimes merge. Setting min_samples too low produces excessive micro-clusters; too high, and genuine small clusters become noise.
Unlike K-Means where poor K choices typically produce recognizably bad results (one cluster containing everything, or obvious regime-splitting), poor DBSCAN parameters can yield superficially plausible but substantively wrong clusterings. Parameter selection requires iterative exploration, often guided by domain knowledge (pollution episodes should contain at least X hourly observations to be meaningful, for instance) and heuristics like k-distance plots that visualize neighborhood density distributions.
The algorithm also struggles with clusters of varying density. If traffic peaks form dense clusters while weekend low-pollution periods form sparse clusters, DBSCAN parameters optimized for traffic detection may fragment weekend observations into noise, while parameters suitable for weekend clustering may merge traffic peaks with transition periods. Real-world data often exhibits this density heterogeneity.
2.4.3 Hierarchical Clustering: Revealing Nested Structure
Hierarchical clustering, dating to the 1960s, constructs a hierarchy of nested clusters represented as a dendrogram (tree diagram). Unlike K-Means and DBSCAN which produce a single partition, hierarchical methods reveal structure at multiple resolutions, showing how small micro-regimes aggregate into larger macro-regimes.
Algorithmic Approaches:
 Two hierarchical strategies exist—agglomerative (bottom-up) and divisive (top-down)—though agglomerative methods dominate practical applications (GeeksforGeeks, 2025c). Agglomerative hierarchical clustering proceeds as follows:
Initialization: Treat each observation as a singleton cluster
Merging: Iteratively merge the two most similar clusters based on a linkage criterion
Termination: Continue until all observations belong to a single cluster
The resulting dendrogram can be "cut" at any height to yield a desired number of clusters, providing flexibility in resolution.
Linkage Criteria:
 The choice of linkage criterion—how to measure similarity between clusters rather than points—profoundly influences results:
Single linkage: Distance between closest points in each cluster (tends to create elongated "chain" clusters)
Complete linkage: Distance between farthest points (tends to create compact clusters)
Average linkage: Mean distance between all pairs of points
Ward linkage: Minimizes within-cluster variance increase upon merging (similar objective to K-Means)
Ward linkage often proves most effective for environmental data, as it balances compactness and separation while optimizing a clear objective function.
Strengths for Exploratory Analysis:
 Hierarchical clustering's primary advantage lies in its exploratory power. The dendrogram visualizes cluster relationships and sub-structure that flat clustering methods obscure. For instance, "high pollution" might subdivide into "traffic-dominated high pollution" and "dust-dominated high pollution," which might further subdivide based on severity or time of day. This nested structure can reveal pollution taxonomies of value to domain experts.
The method requires no pre-specification of cluster number—the analyst can cut the dendrogram at different heights to explore various resolutions. This flexibility supports iterative exploration and hypothesis generation.
For validation of other clustering methods, hierarchical clustering provides an independent perspective. If K-Means, DBSCAN, and hierarchical clustering all identify similar groupings, confidence in those regimes increases. Conversely, if hierarchical clustering reveals sub-structure that K-Means obscures, it may suggest the need for a larger K or reconsideration of regime definitions.
Computational and Practical Limitations:
 Hierarchical clustering's comprehensive approach demands computational resources that scale poorly with dataset size. Classic implementations require O(n²) memory to store the pairwise distance matrix and O(n³) time for naive implementations, though optimized algorithms reduce time complexity to O(n² log n). For datasets with tens of thousands of hourly observations, this computational burden becomes prohibitive.
The method also inherits K-Means' sensitivity to outliers and noise, as extreme observations distort distance calculations and merging decisions. Unlike DBSCAN, hierarchical clustering offers no explicit noise handling mechanism—outliers must be removed through preprocessing or will propagate their influence through merging steps.
Choice of linkage criterion and distance metric introduces additional subjectivity. While Ward linkage with Euclidean distance represents a common default, different choices can yield substantially different dendrograms. Unlike K-Means where poor initialization produces suboptimal but consistent results, hierarchical clustering's greedy merging process means early mis-merges cannot be corrected, potentially propagating errors through the entire hierarchy.
2.4.4 Comparative Synthesis and Algorithm Selection Strategy
Extensive benchmarking studies have compared clustering algorithms across diverse datasets, providing guidance for algorithm selection. Rodriguez et al. (2019) evaluated 12 clustering algorithms across multiple domains and concluded that no single algorithm dominates universally—optimal choice depends on data characteristics. For compact, well-separated clusters with minimal noise, K-Means excels through its simplicity and efficiency. For irregular clusters with noise and outliers, DBSCAN demonstrates clear superiority. For exploratory analysis requiring hierarchical structure, agglomerative methods provide unique insights.
More recent work by Anupam et al. (2025) focusing specifically on geospatial environmental data reinforces these conclusions: DBSCAN outperformed agglomerative methods when noise and irregular cluster shapes characterized the data, consistent with theoretical expectations and the Bárbara et al. (2025) findings.
Study Strategy:
 Given this evidence, this research adopts a multi-algorithm comparative approach rather than committing to a single method a priori. All three algorithms—K-Means, DBSCAN, and hierarchical clustering—will be applied to the Lagos dataset, with performance evaluated through complementary internal validation metrics (silhouette score, Davies-Bouldin index, Calinski-Harabasz index) and domain interpretability.
Based on literature precedent for noisy sensor data, DBSCAN is hypothesized to demonstrate superior performance and will serve as the primary algorithm for final regime identification, with K-Means and hierarchical results providing validation and alternative perspectives. This triangulation approach leverages each algorithm's strengths while mitigating individual weaknesses, producing more robust and defensible findings than single-method analysis.
2.5 AIR QUALITY RESEARCH IN AFRICAN AND NIGERIAN CONTEXTS
2.5.1 The Continental Research Landscape
Air quality research in Africa lags substantially behind efforts in Europe, North America, and increasingly Asia, creating a critical knowledge gap given the continent's rapid urbanization and mounting pollution burden. The research that exists tends toward satellite-based assessments and broad-scale modeling rather than ground-level sensor analysis or source apportionment.
Paterne et al. (2022) exemplify the continental-scale modeling approach, using satellite aerosol optical depth data combined with chemical transport modeling to estimate PM2.5 trends across Africa from 1998-2019. Their work revealed that while global PM2.5 concentrations have stabilized or declined in many regions, African concentrations have increased, particularly in West African nations including Nigeria. This important finding establishes the macro-trend context but provides limited insight into city-level spatial patterns, diurnal cycles, or source-specific regimes that would inform local interventions.
South African research represents the continent's most developed air quality program, with studies employing clustering techniques for epidemiological analysis. Researchers at the University of Pretoria have applied K-Means clustering to identify pollution episodes associated with biomass burning and industrial sources, linking these episodes to respiratory health outcomes (University of Pretoria, 2023). However, this work focuses on episodic analysis rather than comprehensive regime classification, and South Africa's pollution profile differs from West African contexts.
Recent methodological innovation appears in Metzler et al. (2025), who applied deep learning-based clustering to satellite imagery for land use classification related to air quality. While methodologically sophisticated, this approach operates at spatial scales (kilometers) and temporal resolutions (days to weeks) unsuitable for hourly sensor data analysis. The gulf between satellite-based research and ground sensor exploitation remains largely unbridged in African literature.
2.5.2 The Nigerian and Lagos-Specific Gap
Nigeria, despite being Africa's most populous nation and hosting its second-largest economy, produces surprisingly little peer-reviewed air quality research relative to its pollution burden. Much of the existing literature comprises epidemiological studies documenting health impacts or descriptive assessments characterizing pollution levels, with minimal application of advanced analytical methods to understand pollution dynamics.
The Lagos context has been characterized primarily through limited regulatory monitoring data and short-term field campaigns. Researchers have documented that PM2.5 and PM10 concentrations routinely exceed WHO guidelines, that seasonal Harmattan dust events drive episodic spikes, and that traffic represents a dominant source, but these findings emerge from conventional statistical analysis of sparse data (Paterne et al., 2022).
Critically, no published studies have exploited the growing availability of AirQo or similar low-cost sensor networks in Lagos for comprehensive regime analysis. The hourly PM1, PM2.5, and PM10 data across multiple spatial coordinates that these networks generate remains under-utilized. While the raw data is accessible, the published literature lacks comparative clustering studies, systematic regime identification, or even descriptive analysis of diurnal and spatial patterns at the sensor network scale.
2.6 RESEARCH GAPS AND STUDY CONTRIBUTIONS
The foregoing review identifies several interconnected gaps that this study addresses:
2.6.1 Methodological Comparison Gap
Despite extensive global literature applying individual clustering algorithms to air quality data, few studies systematically compare K-Means, DBSCAN, and hierarchical methods on the same multi-pollutant sensor dataset (Rodriguez et al., 2019; Anupam et al., 2025). Existing comparisons typically examine algorithm performance on synthetic data or single-pollutant measurements, leaving uncertainty about relative performance for multi-PM sensor networks capturing PM1, PM2.5, and PM10 simultaneously with meteorological covariates. This study's rigorous comparative framework using multiple validation metrics on actual Lagos sensor data will provide empirical evidence for algorithm selection in similar contexts.
2.6.2 Developing Country and African Context Gap
As documented in Section 2.5, air quality research emphasizes developed countries and increasingly Asian megacities, with African contexts receiving inadequate attention despite bearing disproportionate pollution burdens (Paterne et al., 2022). Methods developed for cities with different pollution sources (limited generator use, different vehicle fleets, no Harmattan) and regulatory contexts (functioning enforcement, available emissions data) may not transfer directly. Empirical application to Lagos data grounds methodological recommendations in African realities rather than imported assumptions.
2.6.3 Visualization and Policy Translation Gap
Academic clustering studies often present results through technical metrics (silhouette scores, dendrogram plots) without developing visualizations accessible to policymakers or stakeholders. The gap between machine learning outputs and actionable policy insights remains substantial. This study explicitly addresses policy translation through multiple visualization approaches: PCA-based scatter plots revealing regime separation, cluster-specific time series showing temporal patterns, spatial maps displaying sensor-level cluster assignments, and heatmaps characterizing pollutant-meteorology signatures. These visual products support policy communication alongside technical validation.
2.6.4 Specific Contributions of This Research
This study makes the following concrete contributions to knowledge:
Comparative clustering analysis of Lagos sensor data: Applying K-Means, DBSCAN, and hierarchical clustering to actual 2024-2025 hourly PM1/PM2.5/PM10 datasets from the AirQo network.


Multi-metric algorithm validation: Employing silhouette score, Davies-Bouldin index, and Calinski-Harabasz index for triangulated performance assessment, going beyond single-metric evaluations common in existing literature


Comprehensive static visualization suite: Developing policy-relevant visual outputs including regime characterization plots, spatial-temporal pattern maps, and cluster signature heatmaps


Empirical regime characterization for Lagos: Documenting the actual pollution regimes present in Lagos based on data rather than assumptions, supporting evidence-based policy development


These contributions collectively advance both the methodological literature on clustering for air quality and the substantive understanding of Lagos's pollution dynamics.

3.1 RESEARCH DESIGN
Research design constitutes the comprehensive blueprint that directs the systematic and rational progression of research activities, providing the guiding structure for selecting methods and procedures for data collection, analysis, and interpretation. The research design for this study is centered on the application of unsupervised machine learning clustering algorithms to air quality sensor data from Lagos, Nigeria. This project aims to address critical gaps in pollution regime identification and pattern discovery through a structured, data-driven approach that leverages modern computational methods for environmental analysis.
The analytical framework is guided by the CRISP-DM (Cross-Industry Standard Process for Data Mining) methodology, a widely-adopted industry-standard process model for data science projects. CRISP-DM provides a structured six-phase approach that emphasizes iterative refinement and validation, making it particularly suitable for exploratory data mining tasks where optimal parameters and methods are not known a priori. The six phases adapted for this air quality study are:
1. Business Understanding Phase – This initial phase translates the environmental policy challenge into data mining objectives. The "business" goal is policy-relevant pollution regime discovery: identifying distinct pollution patterns (traffic-dominated vs. dust-dominated vs. generator-influenced episodes) that can inform targeted interventions. This phase establishes success criteria including algorithm performance metrics (silhouette scores >0.5, interpretable cluster separation) and deliverable specifications (visualization suite, technical documentation, policy-oriented reporting).
2. Data Understanding Phase – This phase involves comprehensive characterization of the Lagos AirQo dataset. Activities include: examining data structure and format (hourly time series with spatial identifiers), assessing data quality and completeness (identifying missing values, sensor malfunctions, outliers), exploring univariate distributions of pollutants and meteorological variables, and investigating preliminary temporal patterns (diurnal cycles, seasonal trends) and spatial heterogeneity across sensor locations. Exploratory data analysis during this phase informs subsequent preprocessing decisions.
3. Data Preparation Phase – Recognized in industry practice as consuming approximately 80-85% of data science project effort, this phase transforms raw sensor readings into analysis-ready features. Key activities include: temporal aggregation strategies to manage computational complexity while preserving informative patterns, missing data imputation using forward-fill and interpolation methods appropriate for time series, outlier detection and treatment to address sensor malfunctions without eliminating genuine extreme pollution episodes, feature engineering to create derived variables (hour-of-day encodings, seasonal indicators, pollutant ratios), and feature scaling through standardization to ensure balanced contributions to distance-based clustering algorithms. The output is a clean, structured dataset ready for algorithmic application.
4. Modeling Phase – This phase implements the core comparative experiment, systematically applying three clustering algorithms to the prepared dataset. K-Means clustering is applied with varying cluster numbers (K=2 through K=8) to identify optimal partitioning. DBSCAN (Density-Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with Noise) is applied with parameter exploration across epsilon (neighborhood radius) and min_samples values to balance regime discovery and noise identification. Hierarchical clustering using Ward linkage generates dendrograms revealing nested pollution regime structure. Each algorithm is applied to the same preprocessed data, enabling controlled performance comparison.
5. Evaluation Phase – This phase assesses clustering quality through multiple complementary approaches. Internal validation metrics—silhouette scores measuring cluster cohesion and separation, Davies-Bouldin indices quantifying compactness-separation ratios, and Calinski-Harabasz scores evaluating variance ratios—provide quantitative performance comparison across algorithms. Domain interpretability assessment examines whether discovered clusters align with known pollution sources (traffic, dust, generators) and exhibit sensible temporal-meteorological signatures. Visualization validation through PCA-based scatter plots, temporal heatmaps, and spatial distribution maps provides qualitative confirmation of regime separation and meaningfulness.
6. Deployment Phase – The final phase translates analytical findings into accessible deliverables. Static visualizations including cluster characterization plots, regime-specific time series, spatial sensor maps, and meteorological-pollutant heatmaps communicate results to policy audiences. A comprehensive Jupyter notebook documents the entire analytical pipeline with executable code, enabling reproducibility and adaptation to other datasets. A policy-oriented technical report summarizes key findings, interprets pollution regimes in Lagos-specific context, and recommends evidence-based interventions targeting identified regimes.

3.3 Programming Methods and Technologies
The implementation employs Python 3.11+ within a Jupyter Notebook environment, ensuring full reproducibility through cell-by-cell execution, inline visualizations, and exported HTML/PDF reports. This choice aligns with data science best practices, allowing seamless integration of code, markdown documentation, and outputs.
3.3.1 Object-Oriented Programming (OOP)
A central class encapsulates the entire analysis pipeline, promoting modularity and reusability across analysis iterations. This class maintains key attributes including the raw sensor data as a pandas DataFrame, fitted preprocessing scalers for feature standardization, and dimensionality reduction components for visualization preparation. Comprehensive methods handle end-to-end processing from raw data ingestion through final cluster evaluation, with results persistently stored for comparison across algorithms. The object-oriented structure enables encapsulation of preprocessing logic, inheritance for algorithm-specific extensions, and state persistence across Jupyter notebook cells, facilitating incremental analysis and experimentation.
3.3.2 Functional Programming Paradigms
The pipeline leverages functional composition patterns through scikit-learn Pipeline objects that chain preprocessing transformations immutably, ensuring consistent data flow from raw features through scaling and dimensionality reduction to final clustering. Vectorized operations in pandas and NumPy eliminate explicit loops, dramatically improving computational efficiency when processing the high-frequency hourly sensor time series. This declarative approach focuses on specifying data transformations rather than controlling execution order, making the pipeline both readable and maintainable.
3.3.3 Parallel and Asynchronous Processing
Joblib enables CPU-parallel execution of computationally intensive grid searches across multiple clustering parameter combinations. By distributing silhouette score computations across all available processor cores, K-Means evaluation across candidate cluster counts reduces from sequential minutes to parallel seconds. Similarly, DBSCAN parameter grid searches benefit from parallel distance matrix computations. This parallelization strategy ensures the full comparative analysis completes within target execution time constraints (<5 minutes total) while maintaining numerical reproducibility through consistent random seeds across processes.
3.4 System Flow Diagram
The pipeline begins with CSV ingestion where pandas automatically parses timestamps as datetime objects for temporal feature extraction. Rigorous quality gates reject datasets exceeding 20% missing observations or containing fewer than 80% complete sensor records, automatically generating diagnostic HTML reports with comprehensive summary statistics. Data cleaning applies median imputation for gaps under 5% and IQR-based outlier removal, conservatively targeting less than 3% data loss. Feature engineering generates 18 derived variables including daily/hourly rolling statistics, PM concentration ratios, binary spike indicators exceeding the 95th percentile, cyclic hour encodings using sine/cosine transformations, and seasonal dummy variables capturing Harmattan versus wet season dynamics. Z-score normalization precedes PCA dimensionality reduction, retaining 95% explained variance in 2D projections optimized for visualization. Parallel clustering executes seven K-Means models (k=2-8), thirty-six DBSCAN parameter combinations, and one hierarchical Ward-linked model. Automated evaluation selects the optimal configuration via maximum silhouette score while ensuring domain interpretability. The final stage produces ten publication-ready visualizations including PCA scatter plots, Folium-based sensor location maps, and cluster-specific pollutant heatmaps.
